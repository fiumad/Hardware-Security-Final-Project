Presentation thesis

Across the generated samples, the most reliable Trojan signal is not a single suspicious token. It is a rare trigger that gates a payload capable of corruption, leakage, denial of service, or silent power/performance damage.

Task 5

Detector design rationale

Heuristic_Trojan_Detector_Plan.txt

1. Extract facts

Inputs, outputs, regs, counters, assignments, if conditions, and suspicious identifiers.

›

2. Score evidence

Separate trigger evidence from payload evidence so benign counters do not dominate.

›

3. Link trigger to payload

High confidence requires payload assignment near a rare trigger guard.

Core principle

A single counter or constant is not enough. A strong Trojan signal is a rare trigger paired with a behavior-changing, leaking, disabling, or high-toggle payload.

Task 5

Scoring scheme

Explainable, additive, static
Score bucketFeaturePointsWhy it matters
TriggerLong counter threshold, e.g. `16'hFFFF`, `>= 255`+3Classic time bomb or rare-event activation.
TriggerMagic constant compare on input/control signal+2Rare activation values are unlikely during normal tests.
TriggerSticky trigger/active latch+2Records activation after the rare condition disappears.
TriggerSequence detector state machine+2Hides activation behind multi-cycle behavior.
PayloadOutput override or forced constant output+3Direct evidence of corruption or denial of service.
PayloadOutput depends on secret/shadow/internal buffer+3Strong leak-information signal.
PayloadHigh-toggle wide register unused by outputs+3Performance or power degradation payload.
PayloadArithmetic corruption, XOR/parity, or leak-like port+2Captures T1 and T2 payload mechanics.
LinkPayload assignment inside or near trigger/active guard+2 to +3Links suspicious activation to suspicious effect.
Risk rule: High when trigger >= 4, payload >= 4, and link >= 2. Medium when total score is high with either trigger or payload strong. Low is a review hint, not a positive detection.
Task 5

Implementation decisions

heuristic_trojan_detector.py

What the detector does

  • Strips comments before scoring, so `trojan` marker comments do not drive results.
  • Infers known labels from `_HT1_` through `_HT4_` filenames for evaluation.
  • Scores trigger, payload, and link evidence independently.
  • Predicts T1-T4 class from dominant payload mechanics.
result = analyze_verilog_file(path) risk = High if ( trigger_score >= 4 and payload_score >= 4 and link_score >= 2 ) detection_pass = risk in {"Medium", "High"} classification_pass = predicted_type == known_type

Why this matters

A naive detector would flag AES constants or UART counters. Our detector requires rare-trigger and payload-link evidence before escalating to Medium/High risk.

Remaining false negatives are possible for Trojans that avoid counters, magic constants, suspicious names, or obvious trigger-gated assignments.
Synthesis

What we learned

Defender takeaways

1. Rare triggers dominate stealth

Long counters, uncommon byte values, and multi-cycle sequences repeatedly appear across T1-T4.

2. Payload class changes the review path

T1/T3 are output correctness and availability issues. T2/T4 may remain functionally silent unless side channels or switching activity are inspected.

3. Explainability is essential

A useful detector must tell reviewers why a file was flagged, not merely return a suspicious-pattern count.
